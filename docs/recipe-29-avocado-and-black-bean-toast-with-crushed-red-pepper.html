Day 29

Avocado and Black Bean Toast with Crushed Red Pepper

A quick toast-based breakfast with guidance on ripeness, spreading, and timing eggs so everything is ready together.

Serves 1 Method: toast Tags: whole-grains, produce, protein

Beginner-friendly instructions

  1. Toast the bread until the surface is crisp and lightly browned. If you do not own a toaster, warm the bread in a dry skillet over medium heat and flip once.
  2. While the bread toasts, prepare the topping in a small bowl. Mash avocado, beans, or other spreadable ingredients with a fork until they are easy to spread.
  3. If the recipe includes an egg, cook it while the bread finishes. Use medium heat so the egg cooks gently instead of burning.
  4. Spread the topping over the hot toast all the way to the edges so each bite has flavor.
  5. Finish with seasonings, fruit, or crunchy toppings and serve right away so the toast stays crisp.

Ingredients

  • 1 slice whole-grain toast
  • 0.5 cup black beans, mashed
  • 0.25 medium avocado, sliced
  • 0.25 tsp red pepper flakes

Equipment

  • toaster or skillet
  • small bowl
  • fork
  • knife

Before you start

  • Set out your equipment first: toaster or skillet, small bowl, fork, knife.
  • Read every step one time before you turn on the heat so nothing feels rushed.
  • Wash produce, dry it if needed, and measure ingredients into small bowls or cups before cooking.
  • If your recipe uses cooked beans, chickpeas, or lentils, decide whether you are using canned legumes or cooking a batch from dry pantry staples first.

Ingredient notes

  • A ripe avocado should give slightly when you press the skin. If it feels rock hard, leave it on the counter for another day or two.

Original recipe card summary

  1. Top toast with mashed black beans and avocado.
  2. Sprinkle red pepper flakes.

How to prepare beans from dry pantry stock

  1. Measure the dry beans. A good beginner amount is 1 cup dry beans, which makes about 2 to 3 cups cooked beans.
  2. Pour the beans onto a plate or baking sheet and quickly look for tiny stones or damaged beans. Throw those away.
  3. Rinse the beans in a colander under cool water.
  4. Soak overnight if you have time: put the beans in a bowl, cover with plenty of water, and leave them 8 to 12 hours. Drain before cooking. If you forget, you can skip soaking, but the beans will usually take longer to cook.
  5. To cook on the stove, put the soaked or unsoaked beans in a pot and cover with 2 inches of fresh water. Bring to a boil, then lower to a gentle simmer.
  6. Cook until tender. Black beans usually take about 60 to 90 minutes after soaking; white beans are often similar; older beans can take longer.
  7. Add salt near the end if you want more control over texture. The beans are ready when you can mash one easily with a fork.
  8. Drain the cooked beans and cool them. Store them in the refrigerator for up to 4 days or freeze in 1/2-cup portions for quick breakfasts.

If something goes wrong

  • Legumes still tough: they simply need more cooking time. Simmer longer and check every 10 minutes.
  • Not flavorful enough: add a pinch of salt, a squeeze of lemon, or a dusting of your favorite spice blend.

Make-ahead and leftovers

Most cooked grains, beans, and chopped fruit can be prepared ahead. Store leftovers in covered containers. If texture changes after chilling, add a splash of water or milk and warm or stir again before eating.
